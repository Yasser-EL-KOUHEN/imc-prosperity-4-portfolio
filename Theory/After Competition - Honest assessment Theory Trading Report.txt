Is it good enough?
For IMC Prosperity specifically: yes, and then some. It is almost certainly more rigorous than what any competing team has produced as preparation material. Most teams at this competition operate on intuition + fast iteration, not first-principles derivations of Avellaneda-Stoikov or Glosten-Milgrom. You have a structural edge in understanding why a strategy works, which matters when you hit a new round with unfamiliar products and have to adapt quickly under time pressure.

For quant finance more broadly: it is a strong foundation, not a complete education. It covers the right things for a generalist — microstructure, time series, basic derivatives, portfolio theory — but it does not cover fixed income, stochastic volatility models (Heston), credit, optimal execution at scale, or modern ML-for-alpha methods. Those are separate textbooks.

What level would you reach after fully mastering this?
Concretely, you would be at the level of a strong first-year MSc Financial Mathematics student who is also a serious programmer. More specifically:

What you would be able to do:

Derive the Black-Scholes formula from Itô's lemma and explain risk-neutral pricing intuitively
Build and backtest a market-making strategy from scratch with theoretically grounded parameter choices
Detect regime changes (HMM, TAR), estimate AR(1)/OU processes, test for cointegration with correct critical values
Read and understand the methodology sections of market microstructure and time series econometrics papers (Glosten-Milgrom 1985, Lo 1991, Avellaneda-Stoikov 2008)
Price and delta-hedge European options, compute Greeks, identify smile arbitrage
Manage multi-product inventory risk with proper position sizing and portfolio delta hedging
Where you would still have gaps vs. a professional quant:

No stochastic volatility (Heston, SABR) — needed for serious options desks
No fixed income / interest rate models (Hull-White, Vasicek)
No optimal execution at scale (Almgren-Chriss permanent impact)
No modern ML-for-alpha (factor models, NLP on news, LOB deep learning)
No real market experience — bots in Prosperity are far simpler than real informed traders
The honest industry benchmark: after mastering this document, you could walk into an entry-level quant research interview at a mid-tier firm and hold your own on the theory questions. You would not yet have the breadth of a CFA charterholder or the depth of a PhD-track researcher, but you would be clearly above an average CS graduate who has "read about algo trading."

Bottom line
At 93/100 the document has essentially hit its ceiling for what a solo competitor can build in preparation for a single competition. The remaining 7 points are either structurally blocked (future round data) or diminishing returns (measure theory for Girsanov, Almgren-Chriss).

The right move now is to stop improving the document and start using it — run the grid search, re-estimate parameters when Round 1 drops, and adapt the strategies to new products. The theory is solid. The edge from here comes from execution, not more theory.
